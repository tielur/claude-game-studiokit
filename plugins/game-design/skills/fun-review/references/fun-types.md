# Fun Types -- Reference

Classification frameworks for identifying what type of fun a game targets. Use when the evaluation requires understanding which player motivations the design serves.

## Four Types of Play

Modern games combine multiple types; the ratio defines the experience.

- **Agon** (competition/skill) -- Player vs. player or player vs. system. The fun is in mastery and proving competence.
- **Alea** (luck/chance) -- Outcomes shaped by fortune. The fun is in anticipation and the thrill of the unknown.
- **Mimicry** (role-play/pretending) -- Becoming someone else. The fun is in inhabiting a fantasy.
- **Ilinx** (vertigo/kinetic/sensory) -- Dizzying, disorienting, visceral sensation. Camera movement, speed, VFX, kinetic character control, rhythm. The fun is in the physical/sensory rush.

### Ilinx as a First-Class Category

Ilinx is the most undertheorized of the four types. Most design discourse focuses on skill, strategy, and narrative, but ilinx explains why an action RPG feels fundamentally different from a turn-based RPG even when the progression and narrative systems are identical. Camera movement, visual effects, and kinetic character control are not polish -- they are primary fun delivery mechanisms for a significant segment of players. A game that neglects ilinx when its genre demands it will feel sluggish regardless of mechanical depth.

### Mixing Ratios

Think of each game as a point on a spider graph across all four types. A fighting game with character customization is heavy agon with some mimicry. A roguelike deck-builder blends agon and alea. Even card games deliver ilinx through VFX and animation. Understanding the mix helps evaluate whether the design delivers on its intended experience. Changing the ratio changes who finds the game fun, not just how it looks.

## Three Types of Fun (Temporal)

Not all fun feels fun in the moment.

- **Type 1: Fun in the moment** -- Immediately pleasurable. Well-tuned feedback loops, satisfying interactions, smooth flow states.
- **Type 2: Fun in retrospect** -- Grueling or stressful during play, satisfying after. Punishing difficulty, survival scenarios, high-stakes competition. The fun comes from overcoming.
- **Type 3: Fun as intentional discomfort** -- Deliberately painful, frustrating, or unsettling experiences players seek out. Rage games, extreme horror, experimental pain-based design. The design intent is the inversion of comfort -- this is a legitimate design target, not a failure mode.

One person's Type 2 is another's Type 3. A player who loves punishing difficulty finds it satisfying in retrospect; someone else finds it purely miserable. This means difficulty and suffering are not universal levers -- their design value depends entirely on who the audience is and whether they trust the suffering will pay off. Players who bounce before the retrospective satisfaction kicks in experience Type 3 unintentionally, which is a retention problem masquerading as a difficulty problem.

## Player Motivation Types

Different players engage with games for fundamentally different reasons:

- **Achievers** -- Want to accumulate, complete, master. Driven by progression and completion.
- **Explorers** -- Want to discover, understand, map. Driven by curiosity and hidden secrets.
- **Socializers** -- Want to interact, cooperate, share. Driven by community and relationships.
- **Competitors** -- Want to compete, dominate, impose. Driven by power over others.

### Matching Fun Types to Demographics

Map dominant player motivations to fun forms: Explorers gravitate toward discovery; Achievers toward advancement and competition; Socializers toward interaction and shared experiences; Competitors toward power and direct contest. Design primarily for the motivations your target audience brings -- don't try to equally serve all types. For indie scope, pick one or two motivation types and serve them deeply rather than spreading thin.

## Social Context as a Fun Multiplier

Activities that are tedious or frustrating in isolation become enjoyable when done communally. Co-op chaos, comparing failure states, building shared knowledge bases, community in-jokes -- these transform the experience. A game's social ecosystem (streams, forums, shared discoveries) is part of the fun delivery mechanism, not just marketing.

Social fun does not require multiplayer features. A single-player game can deliver social fun through a world worth documenting, failure states worth sharing, or a narrative worth discussing. The social fun is in the conversation the game creates, not simultaneous play. Slapping a multiplayer mode on a game that doesn't need one adds infrastructure overhead without social payoff.

## Design Pillars Must Describe Feelings

Design pillars should express the emotions and experiences the player should have, not implementation forms. "Predatory tension" is a pillar. "Third-person shooter" is not. "Kinetic mastery" is a pillar. "Low poly 3D" is not.

The distinction matters because form descriptions don't help you decide whether to add a new mechanic, but feeling descriptions do. When evaluating a proposed feature, you can ask "does this serve predatory tension?" You can't meaningfully ask "does this serve third-person shooter?"

### Baseline Function vs. Design Pillar

A health system is infrastructure, not a pillar. "The player must be able to jump" is not a design pillar for a platformer -- it's a minimum viable form constraint. Confusing baseline function with pillars makes pillars feel useless because they're just restating the obvious. Real pillars are differentiated design choices above and beyond the minimum viable form -- they should feel like decisions, not requirements. If a pillar can't be violated, it's probably a spec, not a pillar.

## Pattern Mastery

Fun includes the brain's reward for learning and recognizing patterns. Games present patterns to decode (enemy behavior, level layouts, timing windows), and the player experiences engagement as they master them. Once a pattern is fully mastered, the game needs either escalating complexity (new patterns) or enough depth that mastery is a long road. Games that can be "solved" lose engagement regardless of polish.

But pattern mastery is one mechanism of fun, not the whole picture. Treating it as the complete definition leads to monoculture design -- see gotchas.
