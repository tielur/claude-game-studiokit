# Fun Framework — Reference

This is the core framework for evaluating fun in game design. These ideas come from experienced game designers and game jam winners.

## The Core Insight

Fun is not juice. A button that sounds good, animates well, and shoots confetti is fun for a moment — but the novelty wears off fast. The real question is: **does the player have a reason to keep engaging 5, 10 minutes from now?**

The answer is incentives: "if I do this, I'll get that." The ability to plan ahead and half-predict what's coming next is where fun lives.

## Five Dimensions

### 1. Incentives Over Immediacy

Players stay invested when they can plan ahead. The loop is: I have to do this so I can do that, so I can do that. As long as the player can anticipate what's coming for them, and you have enough runway for them to keep doing that, they stay engaged.

**Key question**: Can the player see a chain of decisions stretching forward, or does every moment feel disconnected?

### 2. Tension Over Gratification

Games should be more about tension than reward. Gratification is one half of tension — the other half is risk, the prospect of *not* getting what you want. Fun comes from the player knowing what they're trying to do, understanding the stakes, and anticipating what they have to do next to sway the outcome.

**Key question**: Is there something at stake, or is the player just going through motions?

### 3. Multiple Aesthetics of Fun

There are at least three aesthetics that make games enjoyable:

- **Mechanical** — strategic depth, interesting decisions
- **Visceral** — it feels good to play, the controls and feedback are satisfying
- **Fantasy/Escapism** — the game fulfills a play fantasy (feeling powerful, free, clever, etc.)

Game developers often talk about "play fantasies" — the feeling the player wants. A Batman game tunes every design decision toward making the player feel powerful.

**Key question**: Which aesthetic(s) is the design serving, and are decisions being made in service of that aesthetic?

### 4. Surprise Within Established Rules

The best games (and game jam winners) introduce a mechanic, then reveal its scope is broader than the player first thought. That "oh, I see!" moment works because it unlocks new anticipation *within rules the player already understands*. It's like a good joke — something redefines or sharpens your understanding of the play space.

**Key question**: Is there a moment where the player realizes the system is deeper than they thought?

### 5. Player Clarity

The player should always know:
- What they're trying to do
- What the stakes are
- What comes next

As long as that loop keeps developing over time, the game stays engaging.

**Key question**: At any given moment, does the player know what they're working toward?

## Not Every Game Needs Everything

Cookie Clicker doesn't have a twist moment — and it works. Sometimes a fun environment or aesthetic is enough reason for players to stay. Judge the design on its own terms.
