# Fun Framework -- Reference

## The Foundational Mechanism: Flow

Flow is not one ingredient of fun -- it IS fun. When challenge matches the player's skill level, they enter effortless engagement. Too much challenge produces frustration; too little produces boredom. The band between them is where fun lives.

This reframes every design question. "How do I make this more fun?" becomes "What is blocking flow?" Theme, audio, and visual polish don't create fun -- they expand the surface area of flow by engaging more cognitive channels simultaneously. A grey-box prototype with good flow is already fun. Aesthetics amplify it.

## Appeal vs. Enjoyment vs. Fun

These are three separate mechanisms that designers routinely conflate:

- **Appeal** -- why someone clicks. Fantasy, theme, art style, premise. Operates before play begins.
- **Enjoyment** -- enrichment during play. Atmosphere, beauty, comedy, emotional texture. Makes the experience richer but doesn't sustain engagement on its own.
- **Fun** -- the flow state. Driven by incentive chains, tension, and player clarity. This is what keeps players engaged minute to minute.

Fantasy and theme create appeal, not fun. A compelling setting attracts players; a broken core loop loses them. Treating theme as a fun mechanic leads to games that convert interest into churn.

## Evaluation Dimensions

### 1. Incentive Chains

Fun lives in anticipation, not reward. The mechanism: players stay engaged when they can model "if I do X, I get Y, and I need Y to reach Z." The ability to half-predict what comes next and plan accordingly is where engagement lives. Fun is a symptom of this anticipation -- not a cause.

**Incentive chain check**: Can you trace a path from action to reward that the player can see? If the chain is broken or the next link is invisible, engagement drops regardless of how good the immediate feedback is. Juice operates on the visceral layer; incentives operate on the mechanical layer. Adding more of one does nothing for the other.

**Key question**: Can the player see a chain of decisions stretching forward, or does every moment feel disconnected?

### 2. Tension

Games should be more about tension than gratification. Gratification is one half of tension -- the other half is risk, the prospect of not getting what you want. Gratification without risk feels empty. Rewarding players who haven't earned it undermines engagement because the reward has no backdrop of uncertainty to make it meaningful.

**Tension diagnostic -- three questions:**
1. Does the player know their goal?
2. Do they understand the stakes (what happens if they fail)?
3. Can they anticipate the next steps they need to take to influence the outcome?

If any answer is no, the feature produces gratification without tension and will feel hollow.

**The "but" test**: Mechanics with competing objectives signal engaging tension. "Earn money by saving houses, BUT saving requires putting generals in danger." If a design has no "but" -- no competing pull -- the tension is likely flat.

**Key question**: Is there something genuinely at stake, or is the player just going through motions?

### 3. Layered Aesthetics

Three distinct layers sustain engagement at different timescales:

- **Visceral** -- juice, feedback, sound, animation. Sustains attention for seconds to minutes. This is sensory feel.
- **Mechanical** -- incentive chains, goal structures, planning opportunities. Sustains engagement over a session. This is challenge and mastery.
- **Fantasy** -- the feeling the player wants to inhabit (power, cleverness, freedom, dread). Sustains investment across sessions and defines tone. This is imagination and role.

Design decisions should be evaluated against whichever layer they're trying to serve. Don't use visceral tuning to solve a mechanical problem. Don't confuse fantasy with fun -- fantasy creates appeal and enjoyment, not flow.

**Key question**: Which layer is the design serving, and are decisions being made in service of that layer?

### 4. Surprise and Scope Reveals

The strongest surprise structure: establish bounded rules, then reveal mid-game that those rules operate at a broader scope than the player understood. This isn't a narrative twist -- it's a mechanical reframe. The rules don't change; the player's mental model of their reach expands. This works because it unlocks anticipatory potential the player already had, rather than resetting expectations from scratch.

This is distinct from rule-breaking surprises. A twist that violates established rules feels arbitrary and breaks trust. A twist that shows rules work at broader scope feels like revelation. The first destroys the anticipatory model the player built; the second supercharges it.

**Scope reveal structure:**
1. Establish a clear, bounded set of rules in the first act
2. Mid-game, introduce something showing the rules apply more broadly than the player assumed
3. The reveal must be internally consistent -- it expands the player's model without violating established laws
4. Pair it with preceding tension so the "oh, I see" moment releases built-up uncertainty

**Key question**: Is there a moment where the player realizes the system is deeper than they thought?

### 5. Player Clarity

The player should always be able to answer:
- What am I trying to do?
- What are the stakes?
- What comes next?

Clarity doesn't require hand-holding. Stakes can be communicated through level design, environmental cues, and system feedback rather than UI tutorials. The key is that the player has a sense of direction, not that the game spells everything out. But if the player can't answer these three questions, no amount of depth matters -- they can't engage with what they can't perceive.

**Key question**: At any given moment, does the player know what they're working toward?

## Spider Graph Evaluation

Map the game's fun profile across multiple dimensions on a radar chart. This works both for analyzing existing designs and setting targets during development.

1. Choose an axis set (the four play types from fun-types.md, or custom dimensions relevant to the design)
2. Score each axis (high/medium/low or 1-5)
3. The resulting shape reveals the profile -- dominant types, weak types, and potential conflicts
4. For your own game: sketch the target profile early. Does each feature push toward or away from that shape?
5. If two dimensions are both high and pulling in different directions, treat it as a design tension to resolve, not just a feature count

## Feedback Loops

Positive feedback loops (success breeds more success) create snowball drama and urgency. Negative feedback loops (success is counterbalanced) create comeback drama and keep games competitive. Most professional designs pair both types. Check whether the loop type serves the intended emotion: a competitive game with only positive loops will feel hopeless for the losing side; a power fantasy with strong negative loops will feel like it's punishing success.

## Design Depth Check

When evaluating whether a design has staying power, check:
- Can the player prepare in different ways and still succeed?
- Does the environment affect the challenge?
- Can the core mechanics support multiple distinct challenge types?
- Can the player bring multiple abilities to bear simultaneously?

If most answers are no, the game risks becoming solvable or repetitive once the player masters the initial pattern. More content won't fix this -- content without new patterns is a grind.

## Not Every Game Needs Everything

Some games succeed on atmosphere alone. Some are pure mechanical puzzles with no fantasy layer. Judge the design on its own terms. But know which dimensions it's choosing to emphasize and which it's deliberately leaving out.
