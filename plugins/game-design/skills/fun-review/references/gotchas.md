# Gotchas — Common Mistakes When Evaluating Fun

These are failure modes to watch for in your analysis. Avoid them.

## Confusing juice with fun
Juice (screen shake, particles, sound effects) is visceral feedback. It makes moments feel good, but it doesn't sustain engagement. Don't praise juice as if it solves a lack of incentives. Juice is the seasoning, not the meal.

## Treating all games as needing deep strategy
Some games are visceral or fantasy-driven. A power fantasy game doesn't need chess-like depth. Match your evaluation to what the design is actually trying to be.

## Ignoring the planning loop
The most common gap in game designs is that the player can't see ahead. If the design doesn't give the player something to plan toward, say so directly — this is usually the highest-leverage fix.

## Defaulting to "add more content"
When a design feels thin, the instinct is to suggest more levels, items, or mechanics. The real fix is usually better incentive structure with existing elements, not more stuff.

## Overlooking tension in favor of reward
It's easy to suggest "give the player more rewards." But rewards without risk are hollow. If there's no tension — no possibility of failure or loss — the rewards don't feel earned.

## Being vague
"The game needs more fun" is useless feedback. Always tie your assessment to a specific dimension and a specific part of the design. "The combat has strong visceral feedback but no planning loop — the player fights one encounter at a time with no reason to think ahead" is useful.

## Forgetting the player's perspective
Evaluate from the player's seat, not the designer's whiteboard. A mechanic might be clever in theory but confusing in practice. Player clarity is non-negotiable.
