# Gotchas -- Common Mistakes When Evaluating Fun

These are failure modes to watch for in your analysis. Avoid them.

## Monoculture from narrow definitions of fun

Internalizing a single definition of fun (e.g., "fun = pattern mastery" or "fun = dopamine reward for skill learning") leads to unconsciously scope-cutting everything that doesn't fit. You cut beauty, comedy, social texture, atmosphere, and fear because they "don't add to the core loop." The result is mechanically tight but emotionally thin. The pattern-mastery framework isn't wrong -- it's incomplete. Use it to understand reward loops, not to gatekeep what counts as design.

## Defining fun too precisely

The more precisely you define fun, the more types of enjoyment you accidentally exclude. Surprise, comedy, fear, and beauty all drive engagement but don't fit neatly into any single model. Defining fun too tightly changes what you notice during playtests and what you recommend cutting. Taxonomy beats definition for design purposes -- the useful question is not "is this fun?" but "is this the right kind of fun for this game?"

## Confusing appeal, enjoyment, and fun

Fantasy and theme create appeal, not fun. They attract players before play begins and enrich the experience during play, but they don't make the core loop engaging. A game with a compelling setting and a broken incentive chain will convert interest into churn. Treating theme as a fun mechanic leads to designs where the surface is attractive but the experience is hollow.

## Design pillars as forms, not feelings

"Third-person shooter" and "low poly 3D" are not design pillars -- they are implementation descriptions. A pillar like "predatory tension" or "kinetic mastery" helps you decide whether to add a feature. A form description doesn't. When pillars are just genre labels or art direction, they fail their primary purpose: helping you make decisions and reject misaligned features during development.

## Baseline function mistaken for design pillar

A health system is not a pillar -- it's infrastructure. "The player must be able to jump" is not a design pillar for a platformer -- it's a spec. Confusing baseline constraints with design choices makes pillars feel useless because they're just restating the obvious. Real pillars are differentiated decisions above and beyond the minimum viable form. If a pillar can't be violated, it's probably not a pillar.

## Fun is not the hard problem

For experienced designers, fun is often the solved problem. The harder problems are appeal (will anyone click?) and scope (can this be built?). Don't spend the entire evaluation on whether the core loop is engaging if the real risk is that nobody will ever see it or the team can't ship it. Match your analysis to where the actual risk lies.

## Gratification without risk

A game that always gives the player what they want isn't low-difficulty -- it's tensionless. Gratification only registers as satisfying against the backdrop of genuine risk. Removing risk to make the game feel "rewarding" typically makes it feel meaningless. The failure is subtle because the game still delivers payoffs -- they just stop mattering.

## Players see through representation to the underlying system

No amount of polish saves a broken loop. Players engage with the abstract ruleset beneath the fiction. If the incentive chain is broken or the tension is absent, visual flair and narrative won't sustain engagement. The dressing matters for appeal and enjoyment, but the system is what players are actually playing. Conversely, representation is not fully separable from mechanics either -- in some genres, the fiction IS constitutive of the experience, not a layer on top.

## Polishing juice to fix a retention problem

When players stop engaging, the instinct is to add more feedback -- better animations, sounds, effects. But if the problem is a broken incentive chain (no reason to keep playing in 10 minutes), juice doesn't fix it. Juice operates on the visceral layer; incentives operate on the mechanical layer. Adding more of one does nothing for the other.

## Ignoring the planning loop

The most common gap in game designs is that the player can't see ahead. If the design doesn't give the player something to plan toward, say so directly -- this is usually the highest-leverage fix.

## Defaulting to "add more content"

When a design feels thin, the instinct is to suggest more levels, items, or mechanics. But once a player has mastered a game's patterns, more content doesn't produce more fun -- it produces a grind. The real fix is usually better incentive structure with existing elements, not more stuff.

## Praising complexity as depth

More mechanics does not mean more fun. Three mechanics that interact richly produce more strategic depth than ten that operate independently. If a design has many systems but they don't create emergent behavior, the fix is usually fewer, better-connected mechanics.

## Conflating social fun with multiplayer features

Social fun can be delivered by a single-player game with strong community hooks -- a world worth documenting, shareable failure states, a narrative worth discussing. Adding a multiplayer mode to a game that doesn't need one adds infrastructure overhead without the social payoff. The social fun is in the conversation the game creates, not the simultaneous play.

## Assuming Type 2 fun is universally appealing

Building around the Type 2 structure (grueling now, satisfying in retrospect) only works if the audience trusts the payoff and is willing to tolerate the grind. Players who bounce before the retrospective satisfaction kicks in experience Type 3 fun instead -- suffering with no reward. This is a retention and onboarding problem masquerading as a difficulty problem.

## Being vague

"The game needs more fun" is useless feedback. Always tie your assessment to a specific dimension and a specific part of the design. "The combat has strong visceral feedback but no planning loop -- the player fights one encounter at a time with no reason to think ahead" is useful.

## Forgetting the player's perspective

Evaluate from the player's seat, not the designer's whiteboard. A mechanic might be clever in theory but confusing in practice. Player clarity is non-negotiable.
