# Gotchas -- Common Mistakes When Evaluating Appeal & Engagement

## Mechanics carry cultural baggage

Choosing a combat system, interaction model, or genre convention is never a neutral decision. Each mechanic comes with implied emotional weight, pacing expectations, and space for non-mechanical expression. A turn-based combat system implies contemplation and strategy. A real-time action system implies reflexes and adrenaline. A dialogue system implies character and narrative depth. When a mechanic's cultural baggage conflicts with the game's intended tone or fantasy, players feel a mismatch they can't articulate. Evaluate whether the chosen mechanics reinforce or undermine the experience the game is trying to deliver.

## Market-pull mental model

Great marketable products get surfaced. Algorithms and content ecosystems are effective at finding work that deserves attention. The product-to-promotion ratio is roughly 90/10. This doesn't mean promotion is useless, but it has a low ceiling -- promotion multiplies the product's inherent marketability. A highly marketable game with minimal promotion outperforms a weakly marketable game with heavy promotion. When evaluating a design, focus on whether the product has the properties that allow marketing to work at scale, not on whether the developer has a marketing plan.

## Semiotic mismatch

Wrong visual language creates an invisible wrongness that makes players bounce without knowing why. Action-oriented typography on a cozy game. Playful rounded fonts on a horror game. Warm color palettes on a tense survival experience. Players process visual semiotics instantly and unconsciously. If the visual language says one thing and the game delivers another, the result is not "surprising contrast" -- it's confusion that registers as low quality. The developer can't see it because they know what the game actually is. External eyes catch semiotic mismatch; internal eyes almost never do.

## Players buy experiences, not mechanics

The "platformer, but X" pitch structure trains developers to treat mechanical novelty as the primary appeal driver. But players encountering a store page are not evaluating design elegance -- they're asking "do I want to be in that world?" A novel mechanic (a "hook") can work as an appeal strategy, but it shouldn't be the sole strategy. It's a bonus, not a backbone. Test this: if you removed the hook mechanic from the pitch entirely, would the game still be appealing? If not, the game lacks native appeal and is in a fragile position.

## Fun is not the hard problem for experienced designers

After a few years of iterative development, shipping a fun game becomes tractable. What remains persistently hard is making a game players want before they've played it (appeal) and building it within realistic resource constraints (scope). Most developers don't structure their process around the actual hard problems. When reviewing a design from an experienced developer, don't default to fun-focused feedback. The higher-leverage questions are usually about appeal and scope.

## Fantasy and theme create appeal, not fun

Fantasy and wish-fulfillment make a game more appealing (attracting players before they play) and more enjoyable (enriching the experience while playing), but they do not make the core loop more fun. Fun comes from flow. A compelling fantasy without flow-producing mechanics produces a game that attracts players who then churn when the loop doesn't feel good. Confusing the two leads to beautiful, thematically rich games that nobody plays for more than an hour.

## Confusing juice with appeal

Screen shake, particles, and satisfying sound effects are engagement feedback. They make the game feel good to play, which is the toy dimension of engagement. They are not appeal. A perfectly juiced game that looks generic in screenshots is still invisible on a store page. Juice makes someone who already bought the game feel good. It doesn't make someone click "buy." Don't treat polish as a substitute for creative direction.

## Evaluating appeal from inside the design

Designers know their game's systems, lore, and mechanics intimately. This makes it nearly impossible to see the game through a newcomer's eyes. When evaluating appeal, simulate what someone sees in a 3-second clip, a store page screenshot, or a friend's one-sentence description over text. If the design requires explanation to seem interesting, the appeal is weak regardless of how deep the game actually is. External validation is required -- designers cannot reliably assess their own game's appeal.

## Concept strength can outperform execution quality

A mediocre-looking game with a brilliant concept outsells a polished game with a weak concept. At higher revenue targets, concept quality becomes the binding constraint, not execution quality. The belief that "execution is everything" is true at the entry level but has a ceiling. Developers who internalize it may execute well but consistently underperform commercially because they're selecting ideas on creative grounds rather than market grounds. When evaluating a design, weigh concept strength at least as heavily as execution quality.

## Trend identification timing

The best concepts ride trends before they're named. By the time a genre or pattern has been identified and labeled publicly by newsletters and content creators, dozens of teams are already 6-12 months into development. The window closed before the trend was labeled. Look for 2-3 similar successes that don't yet have a category name. Check whether the pattern exists in other media but hasn't been properly executed as a standalone game. If you can see the wave forming but nobody is talking about it yet, there's an information advantage. If you can name the trend, you're likely too late to lead it.
