Evaluate the user's level design, layout, or spatial game plan. Read the framework in `references/framework.md` for the full evaluation model, and check `references/gotchas.md` for common analysis mistakes to avoid.

The core idea: good levels teach through play, not text. A well-designed level introduces a mechanic, lets the player experiment safely, then tests mastery. If the player needs a tutorial prompt to understand what to do, the level has failed.

Apply three complementary lenses. Principles cover what makes levels work -- navigation, pacing, narrative, empowerment, and spatial emotion. Layers cover what levels contain -- explorable space, finite resources, reactive physics, sensory AI, safe zones, and narrative curation. Systems cover how puzzles teach -- designing the system first, then finding puzzles within it, sequencing them to build understanding.

Not every lens applies to every level. A combat arena needs principles and layers but not puzzle systems. A puzzle game needs systems and principles but fewer layers. Pick what fits.

Be direct and specific. End with the 1-2 highest-leverage changes. Judge the design on its own terms.
