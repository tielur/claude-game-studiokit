# Gotchas -- Common Mistakes When Validating Game Ideas

## Skipping External Validation

Level 4 of the Idea Iceberg is the critical gap. Most indie devs handle experience, skill assessment, and market research, then jump straight to building. The hardest question ("would people actually buy this?") gets answered only after months or years of development, when sunk cost makes it nearly impossible to pivot.

## Confusing Personal Excitement with Market Demand

"I love this idea" is not validation. Great reviews plus terrible sales is a common indie pattern caused by ideas that excite the creator but do not convert browsers into buyers.

## Great Reviews + Weak Sales = Idea Problem, Not Execution Problem

A game can achieve near-perfect review scores and still flop commercially. Review satisfaction measures happiness once a player is engaged. Commercial success measures desire to discover the game in the first place. These are different metrics testing different things. If reviews are strong but sales are weak, the problem is almost always the idea failing to convert interest, not the game failing to satisfy players who found it. Do not diagnose this as a marketing failure -- it is a concept failure.

## Sunk Cost Makes Late-Stage Validation Nearly Irrelevant

After roughly 6 months of development, validation data -- no matter how negative -- rarely changes trajectory because the emotional and financial investment is too high. Even a catastrophically bad signal (an announcement trailer that gets almost no views) will be rationalized away. This means the only actionable validation is early validation. If you skip early validation, accept that you are committed regardless of what you learn later.

## Verbal Feedback vs Behavioral Signals Are Fundamentally Different Data Types

What players say ("that was fun!") and what players do (ask for another session, recruit friends, lose track of time) measure different things. Playtesters are socialized to be encouraging and constructive. "I really liked it" and "I'd probably play more of this" are nearly meaningless as purchase predictors. The only reliable playtest signal is unsolicited behavior: requesting builds, extended sessions, recruiting other players. Designing your playtest process around verbal Q&A produces misleading data. Behavior is more reliable than words.

## Validating Mechanics Before Validating Fantasy

Testing whether a mechanic is fun comes after testing whether the core fantasy appeals to anyone. A perfectly tuned loop built around a fantasy nobody cares about will not sell. Players encountering a store page are not evaluating design elegance -- they are asking "do I want to be in that world?" Start with what players want to do or be, then find the mechanics that serve that craving.

## Using a Hook as a Standalone Appeal Strategy

The "it's X but Y" pitch structure trains developers to treat mechanical novelty as the primary appeal driver. But a hook without a compelling experience underneath is fragile. If the mechanic itself does not resonate, there is no other layer of appeal to fall back on. A hook without an experience is a prototype pitch, not a product pitch. Test this: if you removed the novel mechanic from the pitch entirely, would the game still be appealing? If not, the foundation is weak.

## "Build It and They Will Come"

The idea is the single most critical factor in commercial success, more than polish, bugs, or production quality. But you still need to validate the concept before committing, because product quality alone cannot save a weak idea and a great idea still needs to be discovered.

## Testing with Friends and Family

Biased sample. Friends will say "yeah, that's cool" to almost anything. Look instead for behavioral signals: do testers seek out builds unprompted, bug you for updates, put in visible effort? What people say versus what they do are completely different.

## Mixing Genres Without Solving the Appeal Problem

Combining two trending genres feels safe because market research shows demand for both. But the combined audience may not exist. Each genre's appeal makes promises that the other genre's mechanics may break. A strategy game audience expects deep systems; a casual audience expects low friction. Smashing them together does not add the audiences -- it creates a new, potentially tiny intersection. Market research tells you where demand exists, not how to structure a coherent player promise. Mixed-genre games must solve the appeal problem separately: who is this for, and does it deliver on a single clear fantasy?

## Over-Relying on Intuition Without Experience

Intuition is valid at Level 5 of the Iceberg, but only after working through Levels 1-4. Skipping straight to "I just know this is good" without genre knowledge, honest skill assessment, market research, and external testing is the most common failure mode. Intuition is trainable through shipped games -- each completed project, including commercial failures, sharpens judgment about what will work. Early in a career, lean on external validation. Later, intuition becomes a faster and surprisingly reliable signal.

## Ignoring Scope as a Validation Dimension

An idea is only good if the team can actually build it. A constraints-first skills audit eliminates ideas you cannot execute before you fall in love with them. The best indie games often emerge from severe constraints that become the game's identity.

## Survivorship Bias

Studying only successful games produces distorted conclusions. For every game that captured a proven media fantasy, dozens attempted the same strategy and failed. Use anomalies (small teams, outsized success) as inspiration, but weight your analysis toward base rates and median genre earnings, not outliers.

## Genre Ceiling Blindness

Not all genres have the same commercial ceiling. Median simulation game earnings vastly outpace puzzle games across thousands of titles. Picking a genre you love is fine, but do it with open eyes about the ceiling. You can love making games in any genre, so do not stack the odds against yourself without knowing the tradeoff.

## "Some Games Must Be Played to Appreciate"

Pitch decks and trailers cannot capture mechanical appeal for every game. Some games have a tactile feel that is almost impossible to convey without playing them. Recognize when your idea falls into this category and adjust your validation strategy accordingly. Lean heavier on playable prototypes and game jam testing rather than pitch decks for mechanically-driven concepts.

## Bolting Appeal onto a Fun-First Game Late in Development

Starting development around fun and gameplay and then extending a "hook" outward to handle appeal is the typical indie approach -- and the suboptimal one. It means appeal is an afterthought shaped by whatever mechanics already exist, rather than a first-class design goal that shapes the mechanics. By the time appeal is bolted on, the core of the game may not support it. Address appeal from the start, not as a post-production concern.

## Assuming a Gap in the Market Is Safe

Finding a gap is high-reward but also high-risk and hard to verify. It is easy to mistake "nobody has done this" for "nobody wants this." Validating that a gap represents suppressed demand rather than a dead end is genuinely difficult, and this strategy offers less downside protection than iterating on proven formulas.

## Burning Next Fest on an Unvalidated Demo

Next Fest is a multiplier on existing demand, not a demand-creation mechanism. It drives traffic to demos but does not fix demos that fail to convert. If your demo has low retention or poor CTR, Next Fest will expose more people to a broken product without generating actionable learning. Use it after your validation loop has already confirmed the demo works.

## Defining Success After Seeing Results

Define your success metric before you do anything -- before launch, before demos, before development. Post-hoc success definition is how developers rationalize underperformance and continue investing in games that should be scrapped or pivoted. The validation loop only works if you have committed to what "pass" and "fail" mean before you run it.
