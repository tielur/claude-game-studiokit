Guide the user through systematic validation of a game idea's viability before committing to building it. Read the framework in `references/framework.md` for the full evaluation model, and check `references/gotchas.md` for common mistakes to avoid.

The spine of this skill is the Idea Iceberg: five levels of validation, each revealing deeper risks. Most indie devs handle Levels 1-3 (experience, skill, market research) but skip Level 4 (external validation), which is the critical gap. Level 4 is where you answer "would people actually buy this?" before sunk cost makes it impossible to pivot.

Idea quality is the dominant success variable. A mediocre-looking game with a brilliant concept routinely outsells a polished game with a weak concept. Weight your analysis accordingly: spend more time stress-testing the idea itself than worrying about production quality.

Walk the user through each level using their specific idea. At Level 3, assess genre health and concept ceiling. At Level 4, recommend concrete validation steps (pitch decks, itch.io demos, behavioral gap detection). Use fantasy-first framing to test whether the idea's core appeal is large enough to sustain its target market.

Push hard on early validation. Evidence quality and decision utility are inversely correlated across the development timeline. Post-launch data is high-certainty but useless for course correction. Early-stage data is low-certainty but maximally actionable. The goal is to validate when the trajectory can still change.

When evaluating appeal, distinguish between hook-driven and fantasy-driven appeal. A hook ("it's X but Y") creates intrigue but is fragile if the underlying experience is not compelling. A fantasy ("be the thing you've always wanted to be") creates desire that survives first contact with the game. Test whether the idea has native appeal by imagining the pitch without any novel mechanic -- if it is still compelling, the foundation is strong.

Be direct about weak signals. The goal is to save the user months of work on a concept that won't convert, not to validate their excitement.
