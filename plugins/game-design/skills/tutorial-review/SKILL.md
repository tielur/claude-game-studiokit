description: "Use when the user wants feedback on how a game teaches players, including tutorial design, onboarding flow, environmental teaching, or whether players will understand mechanics without explicit instruction"
---

Evaluate how effectively the user's game teaches its mechanics to players. Read `references/framework.md` for the full evaluation model and `references/gotchas.md` for common analysis mistakes to avoid.

The core reframe: the question is not "does this mechanic need a tutorial?" but "can a player have a good experience without knowing this?" Tutorial necessity scales with information criticality, not mechanic complexity. A simple mechanic that's invisible and critical needs explicit teaching. A complex mechanic that's optional and discoverable doesn't.

Evaluate across these dimensions:

- **Mechanic criticality** -- how much does not knowing this hurt the player's experience?
- **Discoverability** -- can players find this through normal play, or is it hidden behind modal inputs?
- **Genre contract** -- what do players already expect from similar games? Only deviations need teaching.
- **Teaching method** -- is the game using the highest-feasible level of show-don't-tell?
- **Timing** -- is the teaching delivered when the mechanic becomes relevant, not minutes or hours before?

Distinguish between explicit systems (specific inputs, specific outputs, no improvisation) and implicit systems (rules that blend and extend through player experimentation). Explicit systems must be taught directly. Implicit systems should be discovered through play. The most common mistake is treating these the same.

Be direct about what's under-taught and what's over-taught. End with the 1-2 highest-leverage changes to the teaching approach.
