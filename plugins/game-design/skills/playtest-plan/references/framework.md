# Playtest Planning Framework — Reference

This framework covers how to design, run, and follow up on playtests. The ideas come from professional studio practice and Jesse Schell's playtesting methods.

## The Core Insight

Playtesting is for making your game better, not for showing it off. Approach it like a scientist designing an experiment: define what you want to learn, control the variables, observe, and analyze afterward. The designer is responsible for the methodology — why you're testing this way, what you tell the tester, and what you withhold.

## Five Dimensions

### 1. Testing Type

Match your method to what you need to learn.

- **Kleenex testing** — for usability, onboarding, and first impressions. Each tester can only be used once because they're "poisoned by knowledge" after their first session. Focus: where do fresh-eyes players stumble?
- **Deep/repeated testing** — for balance, depth, and emergent strategy. The same testers play many sessions so you can observe evolving play patterns. Focus: does the design hold up under experienced play?

Most projects need both at different stages. Kleenex testing is useless for balance; deep testing is useless for tutorials.

**Key question**: What specific aspect of the design are you trying to validate, and does your method match?

### 2. Observation Design

Set up the session so you can capture real behavior, not recalled impressions.

- Watch over the tester's shoulder and take notes in real time
- Players forget frustrations once they push past them — if you only ask questions afterward, you'll miss the most important data
- Set clear boundaries upfront: tell testers what you're looking for, what to expect, and how you'll intervene if they get stuck
- You are the observer, not the host — resist the urge to explain or help unless it's part of your protocol

**Key question**: Will this setup let you see the problems players actually hit, or only the ones they remember to tell you about?

### 3. Problem Recording

Write down problems, not solutions.

- Bad note: "make level 3 easier." Good note: "tester got stuck on level 3 — couldn't find the exit after clearing enemies."
- If you record a solution and it turns out to be wrong, you've lost the original problem
- The root cause may not be what you assume — a tester stuck on level 3 might have missed something in level 2's tutorial
- Collect all data first, then analyze. Don't try to solve problems during the session.

**Key question**: If you look at your notes a week from now, will they tell you what happened, or only what you thought should change?

### 4. Structured Feedback (The Fast Forward Method)

Jesse Schell's questionnaire for getting useful post-play feedback without open-ended rambling:

- **F**rustrating — What was the most frustrating thing?
- **F**avorite — What was your favorite thing?
- **W**ant — What did you want to do?
- **W**and — If you could magically change one big thing, what?
- **D**oing — What were you doing in the experience?
- **D**escribe — How would you describe this game to friends/family?

This works because most players cannot articulate gameplay experiences well. Open-ended "what did you think?" produces either rambling or "it was fun, I guess." The Fast Forward questions probe specific extremes and reveal perception gaps.

The "Doing" and "Describe" questions are especially valuable — they reveal whether players see the game the way you intended. If a minor feature reads as the core of the game, that's a design signal you can lean into or correct.

### 5. Tester Selection and Recruitment

Know who you're testing with and what they bring.

- Collect background data: genre familiarity, experience level, gaming habits
- Core-audience testers may intuit your design or be frustrated by genre deviations — both are useful signals
- Genre-unfamiliar testers face different issues, but their feedback still matters — your game could be someone's first in the genre
- Watch for the "designer tester" who wants to pitch you their version of your game — listen politely, weigh accordingly

**Recruiting approach**: Start with "what's in it for them?"

- Credits, payment, food, the novelty of being involved in game development
- Online: feedback-focused game jams on Itch.io (mutual playtesting), small indie streamers (their audience adds observations)
- Local: schools, colleges, universities with design or computing programs — offer something in return (a talk, a workshop)
- Build relationships in communities before asking for help

## Not Every Playtest Needs Everything

A quick Kleenex test of your tutorial doesn't need a full Fast Forward questionnaire. A deep balance test with experienced players doesn't need Kleenex-style observation. Match the weight of the process to what you're testing.
