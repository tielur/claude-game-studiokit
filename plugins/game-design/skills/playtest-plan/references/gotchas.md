# Gotchas — Common Mistakes When Planning Playtests

These are failure modes to watch for. Avoid them.

## Recording solutions instead of problems
"Make level 3 easier" is a solution. "Tester got stuck on level 3" is a problem. If you only record solutions and they turn out to be wrong, you've lost the original data. Always capture what happened first — figure out what to do about it later.

## Relying on post-session questions alone
Players forget frustrations once they overcome them. The moment a tester pushes past a stumbling block, that experience starts fading. If you weren't watching when it happened, you probably won't hear about it afterward.

## Reusing Kleenex testers
A tester who has already seen your tutorial can't give you fresh-eyes feedback on it again. They're poisoned by knowledge. If you're testing usability or onboarding, you need new testers each round. Save repeat testers for balance and depth testing.

## Asking open-ended feedback questions
"What did you think?" produces either useless vagueness ("it was fine") or runaway monologues. Most players cannot articulate gameplay experiences well. Use structured questions like the Fast Forward method to probe specific dimensions of the experience.

## Treating playtesting as a demo
Playtesting is for finding problems, not for getting praise. If you go in hoping to hear "this is great," you'll unconsciously steer the session away from the uncomfortable signals that would actually improve your game.

## Ignoring non-target-audience feedback
A tester who's never played a roguelike before will face different issues than a genre veteran. That doesn't make their feedback worthless — your game could be someone's first roguelike. Know your testers' backgrounds so you can contextualize their responses, not dismiss them.

## Solving problems during the session
The urge to explain, help, or mentally redesign while watching someone struggle is strong. Resist it. You're the observer, not the guide. Collect all the data first, then analyze it with a clear head afterward.

## Ignoring what testers think the game is
If playtesters describe your game as something different from what you intended, that's a design signal, not a comprehension failure. When they say the cooking minigame "is the game," you're at a crossroads — lean into it or shrink it, but don't ignore the gap between your intent and their perception.
