# Gotchas -- Common Mistakes in Design Discovery

## "Fail fast" doesn't mean discovery is quick

Sometimes enormous infrastructure must exist before the fun becomes visible. Building out ship generation, AI, and combat systems only to discover that the fun was in the moment-to-moment navigation is slow, but it's still "following the fun" -- you just needed enough material to hear the signal. The methodology does not guarantee a short path. It guarantees a direction, but only after sufficient material exists to reveal one.

## Build enough material before listening

You need something worth listening to. A prototype so minimal that it can't produce interesting behavior won't tell you anything. Build enough to generate surprises, then listen. If you treat "follow the fun" as a reason not to build things that don't pan out, you'll never generate enough signal.

## Fixed constraints make iteration faster, not slower

Committing to a theme, a control scheme, or an art style eliminates decision paralysis. Every design question filters through the constraint, reducing the option space to a manageable size. Without an anchor, the search space is infinite and discovery never converges. An anchor doesn't constrain creativity -- it gives the creative process a direction so that disparate discoveries coalesce into something unified rather than scattered.

## Confusing "we found the fun" with "the fun is finished"

Identifying what's working is the beginning of the design process, not the end. The fun needs to be amplified, refined, and stress-tested. Does it survive the tenth time, the hundredth time? A mechanic that deepens with mastery is durable; one that relies on initial surprise is not. Discovery is the starting gun.

## Killing darlings is necessary but mourning is optional

When iteration reveals that a beloved mechanic doesn't serve the game, cut it. But don't treat the cut as a loss -- the information it provided was the value. The dead mechanic taught you what the live one should be. Attachment to the original concept is the single biggest obstacle to following the fun. A game that started as a time-looping stealth game may need to strip stealth entirely when a grab-and-throw mechanic turns out to be the core.

## Over-prototyping without committing

Building ten throwaway prototypes when one deeper exploration would have found the answer. Breadth exploration has diminishing returns -- at some point, commit and go deep. Set a decision point: after N iterations or N weeks, choose the strongest direction and commit. Discovery feeds into production; it does not replace it.

## Ignoring accidental discoveries

Bugs, physics glitches, AI misbehavior, and unintended player strategies are the raw material of design discovery. If players enjoy a bug more than the intended mechanic, the bug is the design. Patching out unintended behaviors reflexively suppresses the most valuable signal a prototype produces. Before fixing anything unexpected, play it deliberately and ask whether it's interesting.

## Structure before identity is costly

Building progression systems, meta-game loops, and content pipelines before the core interaction feels right. Patterns imposed before the game has found its identity force designers to fight the patterns rather than use them. The result: wasted time, stifled creativity, and content that gets thrown out when the foundation shifts. Patterns are only valuable when they crystallize from a game that already knows what it is.

## Treating follow-the-fun as "no planning"

Follow-the-fun is not "build random stuff and hope something sticks." The discovery process requires active design judgment -- recognizing what is interesting, understanding why it's interesting, identifying its implications, and choosing which direction to pursue. The curator role is active and requires craft. Passive play without that judgment produces noise, not insight.

## Confusing player confusion with player engagement

Struggling with controls, unclear objectives, or broken feedback loops is not emergent fun. Players wrestling with bad UX may look engaged, but they are frustrated, not delighted. Distinguish between "this interaction creates unexpected depth" and "this interaction is broken and players are trying to work around it." Watch for signs of mastery and intentionality, not just time spent.

## Solo dev vs team pivot dynamics

Solo developers can pivot freely -- the cost is only their own time and emotional investment. Larger teams need structured pivot decisions. A solo dev recognizing that stealth is not the fun can strip it overnight. A 10-person team with dedicated stealth designers, assets, and systems cannot. For teams, establish pivot criteria in advance: what signals justify a direction change, who makes the call, and what is the maximum pivot budget.
