# Gotchas -- Common Mistakes When Evaluating Polish

## Audio and visual are the same job, not sequential disciplines

Treating audio and visual as separate pipelines that converge at the end produces games where each is good but they don't reinforce each other. Each discipline optimizes independently and solves different implicit interpretations of the game's identity. The fix is a shared theme established early, with both palettes derived from the same constraints. If your evaluation treats audio and visual as separate checklists, you will miss the compounding failures.

## Shared vocabulary problem

Most teams lack vocabulary for audio feedback. Without shared language for attack, decay, sustain, release, EQ, attenuation, and reverb, audio issues accumulate unresolved -- not because they are less important, but because the communication tools are missing. Sound issues persist longer than visual or mechanical issues for this structural reason. When reviewing a game's audio, check whether the team can actually describe what is wrong, not just that something feels off. Establishing even basic terminology changes the feedback loop.

## Consistency IS quality, not a tradeoff against it

The conventional framing treats consistency and quality as competing priorities. The actual relationship is that consistency is the mechanism by which quality is recognized. A game with deliberately limited visuals applied uniformly reads as higher quality than a game with mixed fidelity. An inconsistent high-quality asset reads as an anomaly; a consistent mid-quality asset reads as intention and craft. Do not recommend increasing individual asset quality when the real problem is inconsistent rules.

## Constant maximum intensity is exhausting and self-defeating

If everything pops, nothing pops. Screen shake on every action, glow on every element, epic music in every scene -- constant peaks flatten into a baseline the player tunes out. Impact requires contrast with quieter moments. A game that is constantly at maximum visual/audio intensity trains players to treat that level as neutral, leaving nothing in reserve. Recommend pulling back the default intensity so the peaks actually register.

## Subtlety beats spectacle in audio

Players notice bad sound immediately but only subconsciously register good sound. Audio should feel inevitable, not impressive. The instinct to make sound design prominent is often a sign of a designer trying to make audio "count" in a context where it may be undervalued -- but audio that demands attention pulls players out of the game state you built. Measure audio success by player behavior change, not by whether players notice the audio.

## Polish is not a final pass

Treating polish as "the last step" guarantees it gets cut when deadlines hit. Color coding, audio feedback, and typographic hierarchy are design decisions that affect how players understand and interact with the game from the first playtest. When audio is added after visuals are complete, the audio direction is entirely reactive to visual choices already locked in, and the two never develop a shared vocabulary. Evaluate polish as a continuous practice, not a cosmetic phase.

## All-caps structural readability problem

The problem with all-caps text is not about "shouting" -- it is about eliminating ascenders and descenders, making all letters uniform height. This removes the distinct letter silhouettes that make words recognizable at a glance. This is especially problematic for dyslexic players and in long text passages. All-caps display fonts are designed for 2-5 word labels, not sentences. If the aesthetic requires that letterform style, find a mixed-case version of a similar font rather than applying an all-caps-only font to body text.

## Reference triangulation

When choosing a visual or audio direction, find 3 references from different media (film, music, architecture, fashion) that share the quality you want. Give all 3 to the team simultaneously -- the overlap between them defines the target, and the differences define the range of acceptable variation. If you can't find 3 references from different media, the quality you are targeting may not be clearly defined enough to execute on.

## Placeholder art creates invisible scope

Every placeholder implies a future asset. Teams chronically underestimate remaining work because they stop counting placeholders as tasks. When evaluating a game's polish state, count placeholders to estimate real remaining work. A game with 50 placeholder sounds is not "almost done with audio" -- it has 50 outstanding production tasks that each require creation, integration, and testing.

## Evaluating production value instead of identity coherence

A game with simple visuals, consistent rules, and distinctive audio can feel more polished than a game with expensive assets that do not cohere. Do not mistake high production value for good polish. A clear identity with strict constraints reads as more polished than beautiful but disconnected pieces. Evaluate whether the elements work together, not whether they are individually impressive.

## Semiotic mismatch is invisible to the creator and obvious to the audience

Using a font, color scheme, or sound palette that carries associations from a different genre or era produces a wrongness that players feel but cannot articulate. The creator doesn't notice because they chose the element for its surface qualities without registering its embedded associations. The check: actively research what contexts the chosen font, palette, or sound style has appeared in, not just what it looks like in isolation.

## Font variety without a system reads as cheap

A common instinct is that using many different fonts signals creativity or effort. The opposite is true: audiences associate typographic chaos with low production value. A game using one font rigorously looks professional precisely because it is rigidly consistent. Every new font addition should be evaluated against whether it serves the existing visual language or breaks it. The exception -- varied typography in environmental/in-world text -- only works when it signals a specific aesthetic intent, not when it is the default.
